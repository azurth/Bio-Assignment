Darwin's Evolutionary Impact

Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859. His five core claims still explain how life changes today, from antibiotic resistance to COVID variants evolving in real time.

Darwin's Framework

Five Claims About Life

Darwin proposed five specific claims in 1859. All five still hold up.

Claim 01

Nonconstancy of Species

Species change over time

Species aren't fixed. Their traits shift across generations when some individuals survive better than others. What looks stable is actually a slow-moving process of change. Given enough time, those changes produce something unrecognizable from the original.

Historical Example

Before England's Industrial Revolution, peppered moths were mostly light-colored, which matched pale tree bark. After factory soot darkened the trees, dark moths blended in better and survived at higher rates. Light moths got eaten. Within a century, dark moths dominated industrial areas.

Cook et al., 2012
Modern Example

Antibiotic-resistant bacteria are one of the clearest current examples. When antibiotics kill most of a bacterial population, the few with a resistance mutation survive and reproduce. Their offspring carry that gene. The WHO estimates antimicrobial resistance caused 1.27 million deaths in 2019 alone.

WHO, 2024
Claim 02

Common Descent

All life shares ancestors

Every species alive today descended from earlier species. Trace any two organisms far enough back, and they share a common ancestor. This is why a human arm, a whale flipper, and a bat wing have the same underlying bone structure. They were all modified from the same original blueprint.

Historical Example

Tiktaalik, found in Canada's Arctic in 2004, is a fish with proto-limbs. It had fins that could push off the ground like arms and a neck that moved independently from its body. It's a 375-million-year-old snapshot of the transition from ocean-dwelling fish to land vertebrates.

Daeschler et al., 2006
Modern Example

DNA sequencing gives exact numbers to common descent. Humans share about 98.7% of their genome with chimpanzees and about 60% with fruit flies. The Human Genome Project made it possible to compare genomes across species systematically and trace specific gene lineages through evolutionary history.

NHGRI, 2022
Claim 03

Gradualism

Evolution happens in small steps

Evolution works through accumulated small changes, not sudden jumps. No single generation transforms into something new. What we call a "new species" is thousands of tiny steps that only become visible after a long stretch of time.

Historical Example

Peter and Rosemary Grant spent decades tracking finch populations on Daphne Major island in the Galapagos. After a severe drought in 1977, the average beak depth in Geospiza fortis increased by 0.5mm within one generation because only birds with larger beaks could crack the remaining hard seeds.

Grant & Grant, 2002
Modern Example

Every domestic dog breed descends from wolves. Chihuahuas, Great Danes, and Border Collies share the same common ancestor. Thousands of years of selective breeding produced these differences gene by gene, generation by generation. Genomic analysis shows the incremental nature of those changes clearly.

Freedman et al., 2014
Claim 04

Multiplication of Species

One species can become many

When populations get geographically separated, they evolve independently. Over time they become different enough that they can't interbreed anymore. That's speciation. One ancestral species can split into dozens of descendants, each adapted to a different niche.

Historical Example

The 14 species of Galapagos finches all descended from a single South American finch species. Populations landed on different islands, faced different food sources, and evolved different beak shapes over millions of years. Today each species is distinct enough that they rarely interbreed, even when they share the same island.

Grant & Grant, 2006
Modern Example

In 1971, five Italian wall lizards were introduced to the small Croatian island of Pod Mrcaru. By 2008, the population had evolved significantly different head shapes, stronger jaws, and completely new digestive structures to handle a plant-heavy diet. Measurable, speciation-level changes in under 40 years.

Herrel et al., 2008
Claim 05

Natural Selection

Survival shapes what gets passed on

Individuals with traits that help them survive and reproduce pass those traits to more offspring. Individuals without those traits leave fewer descendants. Over generations, the population shifts toward the helpful traits. It doesn't require any planning or direction. Some traits just work better and get passed on more.

Historical Example

Sickle cell trait is unusually common in populations from malaria-heavy regions of sub-Saharan Africa. Carrying one copy of the sickle cell gene provides partial protection against malaria without causing full sickle cell disease. In areas where malaria killed many people, individuals with that one copy survived and reproduced at higher rates.

CDC, 2024
Modern Example

Red squirrels in Canada are breeding significantly earlier in spring than they did 30 years ago. Researchers tracked this over a decade and found that squirrels with genes for earlier breeding had more offspring as the timing of food peaks shifted earlier in the year due to climate change.

Réale et al., 2003
Case Study

COVID-19 and Real-Time Evolution

SARS-CoV-2 didn't stay the same. From 2020 to 2022, it produced a series of variants that showed natural selection working faster than most textbook examples.

⚡

Mutation Rate

RNA viruses mutate faster than DNA-based organisms. RNA replication has no error-correction mechanism, so each infected cell produces millions of slightly different virus copies. That creates a massive amount of raw material for selection to work on.

🎯

Spike Protein Selection

Most mutations are neutral or harmful to the virus. But mutations in the spike protein that improve how the virus attaches to human cells get selected for because those variants replicate more successfully and spread to more hosts.

🛡

Immune Evasion

Omicron had more than 30 mutations in its spike protein. That many changes let it partially escape immunity built from earlier infections and vaccines, giving it a major advantage over people who thought they were protected.

Viana et al., 2022
⚖

Transmissibility Trade-off

Viruses that kill their hosts quickly don't spread as far. Omicron spread faster than Delta but caused less severe disease on average. Selection pressure on transmissibility was stronger than selection toward lethality, which is itself an example of natural selection shaping the virus.

How COVID Connects to Darwin's Claims

Claim 1 — Nonconstancy of Species

The virus circulating in late 2019 is significantly different from Omicron. The spike protein changed enough that original vaccines needed updates. The "species" changed, exactly as Darwin predicted.

Claim 4 — Multiplication of Species

Delta and Omicron emerged as separate evolutionary branches, not one after the other. They represent the same kind of divergence Darwin described, just happening in months instead of millennia.

Claim 5 — Natural Selection

Omicron replaced Delta globally in a matter of weeks because it spread more efficiently. Nothing directed this outcome. The variant with better transmission simply left more copies of itself.

Variant Timeline
Alpha (B.1.1.7) — Late 2020

First identified in the UK. About 70% more transmissible than the original strain.

Delta (B.1.617.2) — 2021

First identified in India. Became the dominant global strain by mid-2021. More transmissible and more severe than Alpha, with spike mutations that improved binding to human cells. Overwhelmed vaccine immunity built against the original strain.

Omicron (B.1.1.529) — November 2021

First identified in South Africa. Over 30 spike protein mutations. Replaced Delta globally in weeks. Less severe on average but spread fast enough to infect more people in total.

Viana et al., 2022; WHO, 2024
Modern Classification

How DNA Changed Taxonomy

Before DNA analysis, taxonomists grouped species by physical traits. That approach often put unrelated animals together and split closely related ones apart. DNA gives a more accurate picture because it tracks actual evolutionary history. When you compare gene sequences across species, you're looking directly at the record of descent.

Molecular phylogenetics reorganized large parts of the tree of life when it became available in the 1980s and 90s. Some birds that looked like reptiles turned out to be more closely related to crocodilians than to lizards. Whales turned out to be closer to hippos than to other large marine mammals.

DNA Barcoding

A short standardized DNA sequence, usually from the CO1 gene in mitochondria, can identify a species the way a product barcode identifies an item. Hebert et al. proposed this as a method to catalogue all life on Earth. It works on tiny tissue samples and can identify species that are visually nearly identical.

Hebert et al., 2003

Molecular Phylogenetics

Instead of building evolutionary trees from physical traits, molecular phylogenetics uses DNA or protein sequences. Species that share more genetic sequence are placed closer together on the tree. This method overturned several long-standing classifications and revealed unexpected evolutionary relationships across the animal kingdom.

Felsenstein, 2004

Environmental DNA (eDNA)

Organisms leave DNA traces in the water, soil, or air around them. Collecting a sample from a lake or river and sequencing it can identify which species are present without ever catching or seeing the animal. This is especially useful for rare, invasive, or deep-water species that are hard to survey directly.

USGS, 2024

Comparative Genomics

Comparing full genomes across species shows which genes stayed nearly identical across hundreds of millions of years and which diverged quickly. Genes almost unchanged from yeast to humans are likely essential to cell function. Genes that differ a lot between close relatives often explain what makes those species biologically distinct.

NHGRI, 2022
