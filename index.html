Darwin's Evolutionary Impact on Modern Science

Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859, and his five core claims about how life changes over time still explain everything from antibiotic resistance to new COVID variants showing up today.

The Core Theory

Darwin's Five Claims of Evolution

Darwin didn't just say "things evolve." He made five specific claims about how and why. Each one has been tested over and over, and they all still hold up.

1
🔄

Nonconstancy of Species

Evolution Is Real
Before Darwin, most people thought species were fixed and never changed. This first claim is the whole foundation of evolution.

Species are not permanent. They change over time. Populations vary, some traits get passed down more than others, and over enough generations the whole species shifts into something different. That's what nonconstancy means.

Bacteria evolving resistance in real time

Historical Example

Peppered Moths: Before factories, most peppered moths were light-colored and blended in on pale bark. When soot darkened the trees, darker moths survived better and became the majority within a few generations. Kettlewell documented this in the 1950s (Cook et al., 2012).

Modern Example

Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria: Staphylococcus aureus used to die when exposed to penicillin. Random mutations helped some bacteria survive, and those reproduced. Now we have strains that resist multiple antibiotics. The WHO calls this one of the biggest health threats on the planet (WHO, 2024).

2
🌳

Common Descent

Shared Ancestry
Darwin said all life on Earth is basically one giant extended family. Every species alive today traces back to the same original ancestors.

Every species is connected through a shared family tree. The more similar two species are, the more recently they split apart. We share about 98.7% of our DNA with chimpanzees and even 36% with fruit flies. That makes sense if all life came from the same starting point.

DNA is basically the receipt that shows who you're related to

Historical Example

Tiktaalik: A 375-million-year-old fossil found in Canada with fish-like scales and gills but also a neck, ribs, and limb-like fins. It's a transitional form between fish and four-limbed land animals. Scientists predicted where to find it before they dug it up, using evolutionary theory (Daeschler et al., 2006).

Modern Example

DNA Sequencing: Scientists now build phylogenetic trees (evolutionary family trees) from DNA comparisons. Those DNA-based trees match the fossil-based trees almost perfectly. Two totally different types of evidence, same conclusion (NHGRI, 2022).

3
📈

Gradual Evolution

Small Changes Add Up
Darwin argued that big differences between species don't happen overnight. They build up through tons of tiny changes over huge spans of time.

Species change little by little, not all at once. Each small change either helps or hurts survival. Over thousands or millions of years, those changes pile up into something really different from the original. Dog breeding shows this sped up because humans were in charge of which traits got passed on.

All ~400 dog breeds came from one wolf ancestor

Historical Example

Darwin's Finches: Peter and Rosemary Grant spent 40 years watching finches on the Galápagos. During a 1970s drought, finches with slightly bigger beaks cracked tough seeds better and survived. The average beak size in the population measurably changed within a single generation (Grant and Grant, 2002).

Modern Example

Dog Domestication: All ~400 dog breeds came from gray wolves about 15,000 years ago through human-directed breeding. The range from a 1 kg Chihuahua to a 90 kg Great Dane shows how much variation can build up from one ancestor. Whole-genome sequencing confirms it was one domestication event (Freedman et al., 2014).

4
🌿

Multiplication of Species

How New Species Form
One species can split into several when groups get separated and start changing in different directions. That's how we ended up with so many species.

Speciation happens when a population gets split by a barrier like a mountain range or an ocean. Each group faces different conditions, so they change differently. After enough time, they've changed so much that they can't breed with each other anymore. At that point they're officially separate species.

Each Galápagos island was a separate evolution experiment

Historical Example

14 Finch Species: When finches first colonized the Galápagos from South America, flocks on different islands evolved to match their local food sources. Over millions of years one species became 14, each with a differently shaped beak. They can't interbreed with finches from other islands anymore (Grant and Grant, 2006).

Modern Example

Croatian Wall Lizards: In 1971, scientists moved five pairs of lizards to a small Croatian island with mostly plant-based food. By 2008, just 36 years later, those lizards had evolved longer guts with a new internal structure to digest plants. The original population didn't have this (Herrel et al., 2008).

5
⚙️

Natural Selection

The Mechanism
The first four claims describe patterns. This one explains why those patterns happen. Natural selection is the actual engine driving all of it.

Individuals in a population vary slightly from each other. Some variation is inherited. Some traits help individuals survive and reproduce better. Those individuals have more kids, and their kids carry the same helpful traits. Over time those traits become more common. Simple idea, but it explains a lot.

Climate change is acting as a selection pressure on Arctic species right now

Historical Example

Sickle-Cell and Malaria: Having two copies of the sickle-cell gene causes a blood disorder. But having just one copy makes you more resistant to malaria. In regions where malaria is common, that one-copy advantage was enough to keep the gene around even though two copies is harmful (CDC, 2024).

Modern Example

Climate Change: North American red squirrels now breed 18 days earlier than in 1989 because of warmer springs. European great tits have shifted beak lengths. Atlantic cod have gotten smaller as large fish get harvested more. Warmer temperatures are acting as a selection pressure, and the animals are responding (Réale et al., 2003).

Real-World Application

Case Study: COVID-19 Variant Evolution

The COVID-19 pandemic gave scientists a front-row seat to evolution happening in real time, tracked by thousands of researchers across the world at once.

Why COVID-19 Works as an Evolution Case Study

COVID-19 is actually a really good example of evolution because it happened so fast that scientists could watch it in real time. The virus mutates constantly because RNA replication is sloppy. Most mutations don't matter. But a few helped certain variants survive in people who already had immunity, and those spread. That's natural selection, just happening in months instead of millions of years.

🦠

What Is SARS-CoV-2?

It's an RNA coronavirus. RNA replication is sloppy, meaning it makes mistakes that become mutations. Most mutations don't do much, but occasionally one helps the virus spread faster or dodge antibodies, and that version starts winning out.

📊

The Variant Timeline

Wuhan strain (Dec 2019) → Alpha (UK, Sept 2020) → Delta (India, Oct 2020) → Omicron (South Africa, Nov 2021) → XBB, JN.1 subvariants through 2024. Each new dominant variant outcompeted the previous one. That's not random.

🔬

Why the Spike Protein Keeps Changing

The spike protein is what the virus uses to attach to human cells and also what antibodies target. So there's constant pressure on it to change just enough that antibodies can't grab it as well. Omicron had over 30 spike mutations.

💉

Vaccines and Selection Pressure

Widespread vaccination created a new selective environment. Virus variants that could partially dodge antibodies survived better. This isn't a flaw in vaccines. It's evolution doing exactly what Darwin said it would do.

How COVID-19 Connects to Darwin's Claims

1
Nonconstancy

SARS-CoV-2 is not the same virus it was in 2019. Earlier strains are basically extinct now, replaced by hundreds of mutations' worth of descendants.

4
Multiplication

Different variants occupied different niches. Omicron subvariants split into immune-evading and high-transmission lineages, similar to how geographic isolation drives speciation.

5
Natural Selection

Variants with better transmissibility and immune evasion outcompeted earlier ones. Delta replaced Alpha in months. Omicron replaced Delta even faster.

Key Moments in Variant Evolution

  • Alpha (Sept 2020): About 70% more transmissible than the original strain. A spike protein mutation helped it attach to human cells more easily, and it replaced the original Wuhan strain globally within a few months.
  • Delta (Oct 2020): Spread way faster than Alpha and became the dominant global strain by mid-2021. It just outcompeted Alpha for available hosts and took over.
  • Omicron (Nov 2021): Had 30+ spike mutations and spread globally within weeks even in heavily vaccinated populations. Basically showed that the virus could evolve fast enough to partially get around the immunity people had built up.
Classification Science

Taxonomy and Classification in the Genomic Era

Classifying species used to mean grouping similar-looking things together. DNA changed all of that, and confirmed most of what Darwin predicted.

From Anatomy to DNA

For 200 years after Linnaeus created the classification system in 1735, scientists grouped organisms by how they looked. Darwin explained why those groupings existed: similar species share a recent common ancestor. The more similar two species are, the more recently they split apart.

DNA sequencing confirmed this. Scientists build family trees from genetic comparisons now, and those DNA-based trees match the fossil-based trees almost perfectly. Two completely different types of evidence, same answer. DNA also overturned some old classifications. Whales turned out to be closer to hippos than to fish. Birds got reclassified as dinosaurs. The giant panda, grouped near raccoons for decades, is actually a bear.

Genomic Technologies That Changed Classification

Since 2003

DNA Barcoding

Uses a short standardized gene sequence to identify species like a product barcode. It's found thousands of species that look identical but are genetically separate. The BOLD Systems database has over 8 million barcodes for 300,000+ species (BOLD Systems).

Since 1990s

Molecular Phylogenetics

Builds evolutionary family trees from DNA comparisons rather than anatomy alone. It's overturned old classifications and confirmed others. The trees it produces match the fossil record closely (Felsenstein, 2004).

Since 2008

Environmental DNA (eDNA)

Organisms shed DNA into water, soil, and air. Scientists sequence that environmental DNA to detect species without seeing or catching them. In 2019, eDNA found invasive carp in Lake Michigan before the fish actually arrived (USGS, 2024).

Since 2014

Comparative Genomics

Comparing whole genomes across species shows which genes are shared across all life and which are specific to certain groups. This evolutionary approach guides research into disease, development, and genetics (Broad Institute, 2024).
