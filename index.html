Darwin's Evolutionary Impact

Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859. His five claims about how species change still apply today, from bacteria developing antibiotic resistance to COVID variants replacing each other.

Darwin's Framework

Five Claims About Life

Darwin proposed five specific claims in 1859. All five still hold up.

Claim 01

Nonconstancy of Species

Species change over time

Species change over time. Traits that help an organism survive get passed down more. Over many generations the population ends up looking different from how it started.

Historical Example

Before England's Industrial Revolution, most peppered moths were light-colored and blended in on pale tree bark. Factory soot darkened the trees, so dark moths were harder to spot and got eaten less. Within a few generations, dark moths made up most of the population.

Cook et al., 2012
Modern Example

Some bacteria have random mutations that make them survive antibiotics. Those bacteria reproduce while the others die. Over time the resistant ones take over. The WHO says antibiotic resistance caused about 1.27 million deaths in 2019.

WHO, 2024
Claim 02

Common Descent

All life shares ancestors

All species descended from earlier species. If you go back far enough, any two organisms share a common ancestor. A human arm, a whale flipper, and a bat wing all have the same bone structure underneath because they came from the same source.

Historical Example

Tiktaalik was found in Canada in 2004. It's a 375-million-year-old fish that had fins it could use like arms and a neck that moved on its own. It shows the transition between fish and land animals. Scientists predicted where to find it before they dug it up.

Daeschler et al., 2006
Modern Example

Humans share about 98.7% of their DNA with chimpanzees and around 60% with fruit flies. The more DNA two species share, the more recently they split apart. DNA family trees match fossil-based family trees almost exactly.

NHGRI, 2022
Claim 03

Gradualism

Evolution happens in small steps

Evolution happens through small changes building up over time, not big sudden jumps. A new species is just what happens after enough small changes pile up across thousands of generations.

Historical Example

Peter and Rosemary Grant tracked finches on Daphne Major island in the Galapagos for decades. After a drought in 1977, only hard seeds were left. Birds with bigger beaks could crack them and survived. The average beak size in the population increased within one generation.

Grant & Grant, 2002
Modern Example

All dog breeds descended from wolves. Humans selectively bred them for different traits over thousands of years. The result is everything from a Chihuahua to a Great Dane, all from the same ancestor.

Freedman et al., 2014
Claim 04

Multiplication of Species

One species can become many

When a population gets split by a barrier like an ocean or mountain range, the two groups evolve separately. After enough time they can't breed with each other anymore and are considered different species.

Historical Example

All 14 species of Galapagos finches came from one South American species. Different populations landed on different islands and had access to different food. Over millions of years they evolved different beak shapes and stopped interbreeding.

Grant & Grant, 2006
Modern Example

In 1971, five pairs of Italian wall lizards were moved to a small Croatian island. By 2008 they had evolved longer digestive tracts and stronger jaws to handle a plant-based diet. The original population on the mainland didn't have these changes.

Herrel et al., 2008
Claim 05

Natural Selection

Survival shapes what gets passed on

Animals in a population aren't all identical. If a trait helps you survive long enough to reproduce, your offspring probably have it too. Over many generations the population shifts toward that trait.

Historical Example

Having one copy of the sickle cell gene gives partial protection against malaria. In parts of Africa where malaria is common, people with that one copy survived and had more kids. That's why the gene stayed in those populations even though two copies causes a blood disorder.

CDC, 2024
Modern Example

Red squirrels in Canada now breed about 18 days earlier in spring than they did 30 years ago. Warmer springs shifted when food was available. Squirrels that bred earlier had more offspring, so that trait became more common.

Réale et al., 2003
Case Study

COVID-19 and Real-Time Evolution

SARS-CoV-2 kept mutating after 2020. The variants that spread more easily replaced the ones that didn't, which is the same process that drove beak size changes in finches or resistance in bacteria.

Mutation Rate

RNA viruses mutate faster than DNA-based organisms because RNA replication has no error-correction. Each infected cell produces slightly different copies of the virus, which gives natural selection more variation to work with.

Spike Protein Selection

The spike protein is what the virus uses to enter human cells. Mutations that help it attach better get passed on because those variants spread more. Most mutations do nothing, but the useful ones stick around.

Immune Evasion

Omicron had more than 30 mutations in its spike protein. That let it partially escape immunity from earlier infections and vaccines, giving it an advantage over people who had immunity from earlier strains.

Viana et al., 2022

Transmissibility Trade-off

Viruses that kill their hosts quickly don't spread as far. Omicron spread faster than Delta but caused less severe disease on average.

How COVID Connects to Darwin's Claims

Claim 1 — Nonconstancy of Species

The virus circulating in late 2019 looks significantly different from Omicron. The spike protein changed enough that original vaccines needed updates.

Claim 4 — Multiplication of Species

Delta and Omicron emerged as separate evolutionary branches. They represent the same kind of divergence Darwin described.

Claim 5 — Natural Selection

Omicron replaced Delta globally in weeks. The variant that spread more efficiently left more copies.

Variant Timeline
Alpha (B.1.1.7) — Late 2020

First identified in the UK. About 70% more transmissible than the original strain.

Delta (B.1.617.2) — 2021

First identified in India. Became the dominant global strain by mid-2021. More transmissible and more severe than Alpha.

Omicron (B.1.1.529) — November 2021

First identified in South Africa. Over 30 spike protein mutations. Replaced Delta globally in weeks. Less severe on average but spread fast enough to infect more people in total.

Viana et al., 2022; WHO, 2024
Modern Classification

How DNA Changed Taxonomy

Before DNA analysis, taxonomists grouped species by physical traits. That approach often put unrelated animals together and split closely related ones apart. DNA is more accurate because it tracks actual evolutionary history rather than just appearance.

Molecular phylogenetics reorganized large parts of the tree of life when it became available in the 1980s and 90s. Birds turned out to be more closely related to crocodiles than to lizards. Whales turned out to be closer to hippos than to other marine mammals.

DNA Barcoding

A short section of DNA, usually from a gene called CO1 in mitochondria, can identify a species the same way a barcode identifies a product. It only needs a small tissue sample and can tell apart species that look nearly identical.

Hebert et al., 2003

Molecular Phylogenetics

Molecular phylogenetics builds evolutionary trees from DNA sequences instead of physical traits. Species that share more genetic code get placed closer together. It has overturned some old classifications that turned out to be wrong.

Felsenstein, 2004

Environmental DNA (eDNA)

Animals shed DNA into the water, soil, and air around them. You can collect a water or soil sample and sequence the DNA in it to figure out which species have been there. It's useful for finding rare or invasive species without having to see them directly.

USGS, 2024

Comparative Genomics

Comparing full genomes across species shows which genes barely changed over hundreds of millions of years and which ones diverged a lot. Genes that stayed the same across very different species are usually essential. Ones that vary a lot between close relatives often explain what makes those species different.

NHGRI, 2022
