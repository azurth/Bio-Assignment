Darwin's Evolutionary Impact on Modern Science

Charles Darwin published On the Origin of Species in 1859. More than 165 years later, his ideas still explain everything from why bacteria resist antibiotics to how new COVID variants keep showing up. This project covers his five main claims and shows how they still hold up today.

The Core Theory

Darwin's Five Claims of Evolution

Darwin didn't just say "things evolve." He made five specific claims about how and why. Each one has been tested over and over, and they all still hold up. Here's what they are and what they look like in the real world.

1
🔄

Nonconstancy of Species

Evolution Is Real
Why does this matter? Before Darwin, most people thought species were fixed and never changed. This first claim is basically the whole foundation of evolution.

Darwin's first claim is pretty simple: species are not permanent. They change over time. The old view was that every species was created exactly as it is and stays that way forever. Darwin looked at the evidence and said that's not right. Populations vary, some traits get passed down more than others, and over enough generations the whole species shifts into something different. That's what "nonconstancy" means, basically.

Bacteria evolving resistance, right there in the petri dish

Historical Example

Peppered Moths in Industrial England: Before factories, most peppered moths were light-colored and blended in on pale tree bark. When soot darkened the trees, the light moths got eaten faster because they stood out. The darker moths survived and reproduced more. Within a few generations, dark moths became the majority. Bernard Kettlewell studied this in the 1950s and it became one of the first documented examples of evolution happening in real time (Cook et al., 2012).

Modern Example

Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria: Staphylococcus aureus used to die when exposed to penicillin. Then some bacteria had random mutations that helped them survive. Those bacteria reproduced. Now we have strains that resist multiple antibiotics and are way harder to treat. The WHO lists this as one of the biggest health threats on the planet right now (WHO, 2024). That's nonconstancy. The species changed.

2
🌳

Common Descent

Shared Ancestry
This one is kind of mind-blowing. Darwin said all life on Earth is basically one giant extended family. Every species alive today traces back to the same original ancestors.

Common descent means that every species on Earth is connected through a shared family tree. The more similar two species are, the more recently they split apart on that tree. Darwin figured this out mostly from fossils and anatomy. But now DNA sequencing makes it incredibly clear. We share about 98.7% of our DNA with chimpanzees. We share 36% with fruit flies. Even that seems wild, but it makes complete sense if all life came from the same starting point billions of years ago.

DNA is basically the receipt that shows who you're related to

Historical Example

Tiktaalik and the Fossil Record: In 2004, scientists found a 375-million-year-old fossil called Tiktaalik roseae in Canada. It had fish features like scales and gills but also had a neck, ribs, and stubby fins that worked almost like limbs. It was a transitional fossil, meaning it shows the shift between fish and four-limbed land animals. The really cool part is that scientists predicted where to find it before they dug it up, based on evolutionary theory (Daeschler et al., 2006).

Modern Example

DNA Sequencing and Family Trees: Scientists now build phylogenetic trees (basically evolutionary family trees) using DNA comparisons. And here's the thing: those DNA-based trees match the fossil-based trees almost perfectly. Two completely different types of evidence pointing to the same conclusion. That's about as solid as scientific evidence gets (National Human Genome Research Institute, 2022).

3
📈

Gradual Evolution

Small Changes Add Up
Darwin argued that big differences between species don't happen overnight. They build up slowly through tons of tiny changes over huge spans of time.

Gradual evolution is the idea that species change little by little, not all at once. Each small change either helps or hurts survival. Helpful ones stick around. Over thousands or millions of years, those small changes pile up into something really different from the original. Dog breeding is a good way to see this sped up. Humans basically ran an evolution experiment by selectively breeding dogs for specific traits, and the results are pretty extreme if you compare a Chihuahua to a Great Dane. Same process, just faster because we were in charge.

All ~400 dog breeds came from one wolf ancestor through gradual change

Historical Example

Darwin's Finches: Peter and Rosemary Grant spent 40 years watching finches on the Galápagos island Daphne Major. During a drought in the 1970s, small soft seeds ran out and only large tough seeds were left. Finches with slightly bigger beaks could crack those seeds. They survived. The smaller-beaked birds mostly didn't. The average beak size in the whole population measurably changed within a single generation. That's gradual evolution you can actually watch (Grant and Grant, 2002).

Modern Example

Dog Domestication: All ~400 recognized domestic dog breeds came from gray wolves roughly 15,000 years ago. Humans bred them for specific jobs, looks, or temperaments, and that selective pressure produced an almost unbelievable range of variation. Whole-genome sequencing confirms they all share one domestication event (Freedman et al., 2014). Same wolf, totally different outcomes.

4
🌿

Multiplication of Species

How New Species Form
So if species change over time, how do we end up with so many of them? This claim explains that. One species can split into several when groups get separated and start changing in different directions.

Speciation is the scientific word for when one species becomes two or more. It usually happens when a population gets split up by some kind of barrier, like a mountain range, an ocean, or even just a lot of distance. Each group faces different conditions, so they change in different ways. After enough time, they've changed so much that they can't breed with each other anymore. At that point, they're officially separate species. The Galápagos are basically the most famous example of this ever.

Each Galápagos island was like a separate evolution experiment

Historical Example

14 Finch Species from One Ancestor: When finches first landed on the Galápagos from South America, different flocks ended up on different islands. Each island had different food. Over millions of years, the populations changed to match their island's resources until they couldn't interbreed with the other islands' finches anymore. One species became 14. That's speciation in action (Grant and Grant, 2006).

Modern Example

Italian Wall Lizards in Croatia: In 1971, scientists put five pairs of lizards on a small Croatian island that was mostly plants instead of insects. By 2008, just 36 years later, those lizards had evolved longer guts with a new internal structure that helps them digest plants. The original population didn't have this. New environment, new diet, new anatomy (Herrel et al., 2008). That's fast, too.

5
⚙️

Natural Selection

The Mechanism
The first four claims describe patterns. This one explains why those patterns happen. Natural selection is the actual engine driving all of it.

Natural selection is probably the most famous part of Darwin's theory, and for good reason. It's the mechanism that drives everything else. Here's how it works: individuals in a population vary slightly from each other. Some of that variation is inherited. Some traits help individuals survive and reproduce better than others. Those individuals have more kids, and their kids carry the same helpful traits. Over time, those traits become more common in the whole population. That's it. Simple idea, but it explains a lot.

Climate change is acting as a selection pressure on Arctic species right now

Historical Example

Sickle-Cell Anemia and Malaria: Having two copies of the sickle-cell gene causes a painful blood disorder. So you'd think the gene would get selected out. But in regions with a lot of malaria, people with one copy of the gene are actually more resistant to malaria than people with no copies. So the gene stuck around because in those environments, having one copy gives you a survival edge. That's balancing selection, and it's a really good example of how natural selection isn't simple (CDC, 2024).

Modern Example

Climate Change Reshaping Species: North American red squirrels are breeding about 18 days earlier in the year than they did in 1989, because warmer springs mean earlier food. European great tits have shifted beak lengths. Atlantic cod have gotten smaller as large fish get fished out more. These aren't random. Warmer temperatures and changed conditions are acting as selection pressures. The animals whose traits fit the new environment survive better (Réale et al., 2003).

Real-World Application

Case Study: COVID-19 Variant Evolution

The COVID-19 pandemic gave scientists a front-row seat to evolution happening in real time, tracked by thousands of researchers across the entire world at once.

Why COVID-19 Works as an Evolution Case Study

SARS-CoV-2 (that's the formal name for the COVID-19 virus) has a genome of about 30,000 RNA bases and can replicate in hours. RNA viruses also don't proofread their copies like DNA does, so they mutate fast. Between 2019 and 2024, the virus generated thousands of variants. Most were neutral. A few had mutations that helped the virus survive in hosts with immunity. Those ones spread. The whole thing is basically a live demonstration of natural selection at pandemic scale.

🦠

What Is SARS-CoV-2?

It's a type of coronavirus that uses RNA instead of DNA to store its genetic information. RNA replication is sloppy, meaning it makes mistakes. Those mistakes are mutations. Most mutations don't do much. But occasionally one mutation helps the virus spread more easily or dodge antibodies, and that version starts winning out.

📊

The Variant Timeline

Wuhan strain (Dec 2019) → Alpha in the UK (Sept 2020) → Beta in South Africa (Oct 2020) → Delta in India (Oct 2020) → Omicron in southern Africa (Nov 2021) → XBB, BQ.1, JN.1 subvariants through 2024. Each new dominant variant outcompeted the previous one. That's not random.

🔬

Why the Spike Protein Keeps Changing

The spike protein is what the virus uses to attach to human cells. It's also what antibodies target. So there's constant pressure on the spike protein to change just enough that antibodies can't grab it as well. Omicron had over 30 spike mutations. It basically ran through the immune defenses most people had built up against earlier strains.

💉

Vaccines and Selection Pressure

Widespread vaccination created a new selective environment. Virus variants that could partially dodge antibodies survived better and had more offspring. This isn't a flaw in vaccines, it's evolution doing exactly what Darwin said it would. The most important lesson here is that understanding evolution helps us predict and respond to viral threats faster.

How COVID-19 Connects to Darwin's Claims

1
Nonconstancy

SARS-CoV-2 is not the same virus it was in 2019. Earlier strains are basically extinct now, replaced by hundreds of mutations worth of descendants. The virus that caused the first wave looked pretty different from Omicron. That's exactly what nonconstancy predicts: given enough time and selection pressure, a species changes.

4
Multiplication

Different variants spread best in different situations. Some were better at infecting vaccinated people, others spread faster in unvaccinated populations. They weren't competing in different geographic locations like Darwin's finches, but the idea is the same: one population splitting into groups that are each better suited to a different environment.

5
Natural Selection

Variants with better transmissibility and immune evasion outcompeted the old ones. Delta replaced Alpha in a matter of months. Omicron replaced Delta even faster. The variants that survived weren't "trying" to get better at spreading. They just happened to have mutations that worked, and natural selection took care of the rest.

Timeline of Major Variant Events

December 2019

Ancestral Wuhan Strain

First identified in Wuhan, China. Genome shared publicly on January 10, 2020, which let scientists worldwide start tracking changes from day one.

September 2020

Alpha Variant (B.1.1.7)

Showed up in Kent, UK. About 70% more transmissible than the original strain. A mutation in the spike protein made it stick to human cells more easily, which gave it a clear advantage over the original strain.

October 2020

Delta Variant (B.1.617.2)

Came out of India. Became the dominant global strain by mid-2021. Roughly doubled viral loads in infected people. It just outcompeted Alpha for available hosts and took over.

November 2021

Omicron (B.1.1.529)

Emerged in southern Africa with 30+ spike mutations. Became globally dominant within weeks even in heavily vaccinated populations. Showed that evolution can work around human-engineered immunity when given enough time and hosts.

2022 to 2024

Continued Diversification

XBB, BQ.1, EG.5, JN.1, KP and more. The virus keeps diversifying into available niches. The WHO tracks it all with real-time phylogenetic analysis, which only works because of Darwin's framework.

Classification Science

Taxonomy and Classification in the Genomic Era

Classifying species used to mean looking at physical features and grouping similar-looking things together. DNA changed all of that, and confirmed most of what Darwin predicted.

From Looking at Anatomy to Reading DNA

For about 200 years after Linnaeus invented the classification system in 1735, scientists grouped organisms by how they looked. Shape of bones, structure of leaves, body plan. Darwin came along and explained why those groupings existed: similar species are grouped together because they share a recent common ancestor. The more similar two species are, the more recently they split apart on the evolutionary tree.

Then DNA sequencing arrived and things got really interesting. Scientists could now compare the actual genetic code of different species and build family trees based on how much of that code they share. And what happened? The DNA-based trees matched the fossil-based trees almost perfectly. Two completely different lines of evidence, same conclusion.

But DNA also overturned some old classifications. Whales used to be grouped separately from land mammals. Genomic data showed they're actually even-toed ungulates, closer to hippos than to anything that lives in the sea all its life. Birds got reclassified as avian dinosaurs. The giant panda, which was classified near raccoons for decades, turned out to be a bear.

DNA barcoding, introduced by Paul Hebert in 2003, takes this even further. It uses a short, standardized piece of DNA to identify species kind of like a barcode on a product. The BOLD database now has over 8 million barcodes for 300,000 species. It's used to catch food fraud, detect invasive species, and identify organisms in conservation work.

Each level represents a branching point in evolutionary history. Our placement in family Hominidae alongside gorillas and chimps reflects about 6 to 8 million years of shared ancestry, confirmed by both the fossil record and genomic sequencing.

Genomic Technologies That Changed Classification

Since 2003

DNA Barcoding

Uses a short, standardized gene sequence (the COI gene in animals) to identify species, kind of like a product barcode. It's found thousands of species that look identical but are genetically separate. The BOLD Systems database has 8 million plus barcodes for over 300,000 species, used in food fraud detection, invasive species tracking, and conservation (BOLD Systems).

Since 1990s

Molecular Phylogenetics

Builds evolutionary family trees from DNA comparisons rather than anatomy. Overturned a bunch of old classifications. Whales turned out to be ungulates. Birds are dinosaurs. Both "pandas" aren't actually closely related. The DNA-based trees match fossil-based trees closely, which is strong evidence that both are tracking the same real history (Felsenstein, 2004).

Since 2008

Environmental DNA (eDNA)

Organisms shed DNA into water, soil, and air. Scientists can now sequence that environmental DNA to detect species without seeing or catching them. In 2019, eDNA surveillance found invasive Asian carp DNA in Lake Michigan before the fish themselves showed up. That kind of early detection is only possible because of how well we understand evolutionary genetics (USGS, 2024).

Since 2014

Comparative Genomics

Comparing whole genomes across species shows which genes are shared across all life, which are shared across vertebrates, and which are specific to primates or humans. This evolutionary approach guides research into disease, development, and genetics. It only works because Darwin gave us the framework to understand why similar genes show up across very different species (Broad Institute, 2024).
